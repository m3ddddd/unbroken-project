Tab 01: Historical Record

THE HISTORY

From the Jim Crow era to the Civil Rights Movement: a record of struggle, survival, and the fight for equality in American education and society.

1865
Post-Slavery & Early Discrimination
After the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, Black Americans faced systematic exclusion through Black Codes and early segregation. The Reconstruction era promised equality but was quickly dismantled by white supremacist violence and political rollback.
"The end of slavery did not mean the beginning of freedom — it meant the beginning of a different kind of bondage: economic, legal, and social."
Source: Howard Law Library, Civil Rights History ↗
1877
Jim Crow Laws Take Hold
Following the end of Reconstruction, Southern states enacted "Jim Crow" laws mandating racial segregation in schools, public transportation, restaurants, restrooms, and nearly every aspect of public life. Interracial marriage was outlawed. Voting rights were stripped through literacy tests and poll taxes.
"Separate but equal" became the law of the land, but the separation was real and the equality was a lie.
Source: Howard Law Library, Jim Crow ↗
1896
Plessy v. Ferguson
The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in Plessy v. Ferguson that racial segregation was constitutional under the "separate but equal" doctrine. This decision legally cemented segregation for nearly 60 years and became the foundation for discrimination across every American institution.
"The arbitrary separation of citizens on the basis of race... is a badge of servitude wholly inconsistent with the civil freedom and the equality before the law." — Justice John Harlan, dissenting
Source: National Archives, Brown v. Board ↗
1954
Brown v. Board of Education
In a unanimous ruling, the Supreme Court declared that racial segregation in public schools was unconstitutional, overturning Plessy v. Ferguson. Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." The ruling sparked massive resistance in the South and galvanized the Civil Rights Movement.
"Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." — Chief Justice Earl Warren, Brown v. Board (1954)
Source: National Archives, archives.gov ↗
1955–65
The Civil Rights Movement
Sparked by Rosa Parks' arrest, the Montgomery Bus Boycott launched a decade of direct action. Sit-ins, Freedom Rides, and the March on Washington followed. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965 dismantled the legal structure of Jim Crow, but systemic inequality persisted.
"Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere." — Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
Source: Smithsonian NMAAHC, nmaahc.si.edu ↗
1964
NYC School Boycott: Student Power
On February 3, 1964, nearly half a million New York City students (the largest civil rights demonstration in U.S. history) boycotted school to protest segregated and underfunded Black schools. Organized by parents and activists, the boycott demonstrated that students themselves were powerful agents of change.
"The children of New York walked out not in anger but in clarity — they understood that an unequal education was a stolen future."
Source: TIME Magazine, "Nearly Half of NYC Students Boycotted Schools" ↗

Education Inequality
Decades after Brown v. Board, Black and Latino students are still concentrated in underfunded schools.
School funding in the U.S. is largely based on local property taxes, meaning wealthier (often whiter) neighborhoods have far better-resourced schools. A 2024 AP News report found that even with school choice programs, many Black families still face limited options. The NAACP sued the U.S. Education Department in April 2025 over cuts to DEI school funding, arguing these cuts directly harm students of color.
"Choice without equity is just choosing your inequality." — NAACP Education Brief

Policing & Discrimination
Black Americans face disproportionate rates of police contact, use-of-force incidents, and incarceration.
Pew Research Center data consistently shows Black Americans are significantly more likely to be stopped, searched, or use-of-force applied against them by police compared to white Americans with similar circumstances. Black men are incarcerated at five times the rate of white men. These disparities trace directly to the legacy of criminalization built during Jim Crow and upheld through decades of discriminatory policy.
"The system is not broken. It was built this way." — Bryan Stevenson, Equal Justice Initiative

Housing & Economic Gaps
Redlining's legacy still shapes where families live, what schools they can access, and what wealth they can build.
The practice of redlining (government-backed denial of mortgages to Black families in the mid-20th century) stripped generations of wealth. Today, the racial wealth gap persists: the median white family holds roughly 8× the wealth of the median Black family. Neighborhoods that were redlined in the 1940s are still lower-income today, and the schools in those neighborhoods still receive less funding.
"Redlining didn't just deny homes — it denied generational wealth, good schools, and safety." — Pew Research

Tab 03: Voices & Testimonies

THE STORIES

Behind every statistic is a person. Behind every law is a protest. Behind every protest is a student who decided enough was enough.

Historical Event · 1964
The Largest School Boycott in U.S. History
On February 3, 1964, approximately 464,000 students, nearly half of New York City's public school enrollment, stayed home or attended "freedom schools" to protest segregated, overcrowded, and underfunded Black schools. Organized by the Citywide Committee for Integrated Schools, the boycott was larger than any single civil rights march. Students and families demanded real integration, not just the legal fiction of desegregation.
"The children proved that day that they understood something many adults refused to acknowledge: that their education was being deliberately sabotaged."
Source: TIME Magazine: "Nearly Half of NYC Students Boycotted Schools to Protest Segregation"
Youth Activism · Today
NAACP Youth & College Division: Students Still Fighting
The NAACP Youth & College Division, founded in 1936, remains one of the most powerful student organizing bodies in the country. High school and college students today organize voter registration drives, protest police brutality, fight for inclusive curricula, and advocate for equitable school funding. The same spirit that drove the 1964 boycott lives in students across America today.
"Every generation has to fight for the same rights — but every generation also gets to decide how far they push."
Source: NAACP Youth and College, naacp.org
Living History · Modern
School Choice Hasn't Fixed What Segregation Broke
A 2024 AP News investigation found that despite expanded school choice programs in many cities, Black families often still find themselves with limited quality options. Transportation barriers, application processes, admissions criteria, and resource gaps mean that the promise of choice does not always translate to equity. Many families described feeling like they were navigating a system designed to keep them out, not welcome them in.
"We were told we could choose. But the options we were given were all the same — underfunded, overcrowded, and overlooked." — Parent interviewed by AP News
Source: AP News: "Even with school choice, some Black families find options lacking"
Smithsonian Archive
The Struggle Against Segregated Education
The Smithsonian's National Museum of African American History and Culture documents the decades of resistance against segregated schools, from the courage of the Little Rock Nine in 1957 (nine Black students who entered an all-white Arkansas school under military escort) to the Freedom Schools of Mississippi in 1964 that taught Black children their history and rights when public schools refused to. Education itself became a battleground.
"Education has always been the civil rights battleground — because those in power know that an educated, informed people cannot be controlled."
Source: Smithsonian NMAAHC: Struggle Against Segregated Education

Tab 04: Taking Action

HOW TO RESIST

Knowing history is not enough. It must become action. Here is how students can use that knowledge to fight injustice.

How does knowing history inform your ability to resist injustice?
History reveals the patterns of oppression: how power is maintained, how rights are stripped, and how ordinary people organized to push back. When you know that the 1964 NYC school boycott was organized by parents and students, not politicians, you understand that change comes from people like you. When you know that Jim Crow laws required massive public pressure to dismantle, you understand why silence is never neutral. History is not background information. It is a blueprint for action.

THE HISTORICAL → PRESENT CONNECTION

→
Jim Crow laws denied education → Today, underfunded schools in non-white districts continue that denial. Knowing this history means you can name the pattern and demand change.
→
The 1964 boycott worked → because students showed up, organized, and made their voices impossible to ignore. That same strategy works today: walkouts, petitions, school board testimony.
→
Curriculum erasure is not new → Post-Reconstruction, Black history was systematically removed from textbooks. Understanding this history means fighting to protect and expand what is taught in schools today.
→
Brown v. Board required years of legal organizing → The NAACP built a decades-long litigation strategy. Systemic change requires sustained effort, not a single moment.
01
Demand Honest History Curriculum
Advocate for accurate African American history in your school. Attend school board meetings. Request that your teachers use primary sources. Support organizations fighting curriculum erasure.
